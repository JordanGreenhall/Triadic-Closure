# CH2 — Two-Center Capacity, Ownership, and Mode Fork

## Standing summary

CH2 normalizes the bounded internal consequences of the CH1 candidate joint-standing bond form. It does not establish chemistry entry, a bond occupant, energetic stabilization, or CH3 maintenance-relief.

Three results are separated:

1. **capacity:** one shared spatial mode has capacity two under the inherited antisymmetric spin holding, while occupancy may be one;
2. **ownership:** the shared standing belongs to the composite; constituent assignment is basis-relative unless registered by an actual passage;
3. **mode fork:** two interchangeable center basis states yield symmetric and antisymmetric combinations; `n` independent center states yield `n` linear modes.

The capacity and mode-count results are **Secured conditionally** and **Registered at their bounded mathematical/physical scope**. Composite ownership is **Conjectured-strong** conditional on CH1 and remains Unregistered as a completed physical ownership theorem.

## 1. Capacity is not occupancy

Let one shared spatial mode be available to indistinguishable fermionic occupants under the inherited antisymmetric spin holding. The admitted paired spin states give capacity two for that mode. This is a capacity bound, not an occupancy theorem.

Therefore:

- occupancy zero, one, or two is not decided by capacity alone;
- a one-electron bond candidate is not excluded;
- observed filling patterns, energetic preference, and electron count require separate premises.

## 2. Composite ownership

Under CH1's selected joint-standing form, the shared pair or single occupant participates in one composite closure. A decomposition into “electron on center A” and “electron on center B” is a choice of basis or bookkeeping until an actual passage or registered distinction makes constituent assignment determinate.

This claim does not deny localized measurement outcomes. It distinguishes the held composite standing from a later registered constituent-relative result.

## 3. Two-center mode fork

For two interchangeable center basis states `|A>` and `|B>`, the combinations

`|+> = (|A> + |B>)/sqrt(2)`

and

`|-> = (|A> - |B>)/sqrt(2)`

span the same two-dimensional space and diagonalize center interchange. The fork is exactly two because the center basis has dimension two.

For `n` independent center basis states, linear combination yields an `n`-dimensional mode space. The two-mode result must not be generalized as a universal chemistry duality.

Nothing in this linear algebra fixes energy ordering, occupancy, stabilization, or the labels “bonding” and “antibonding” as physical results.
